Community Health Worker Certification Act

HB 1234 — 89th Texas Legislature

Legislative Timeline

Jan 15, 2026Introduced by Rep. John Smith
Feb 3, 2026Co-sponsored by Sen. Jane Doe
Feb 20, 2026Referred to Health Committee

Summary

This bill establishes a state certification program for community health workers, ensuring standardized training, accountability, and sustainable funding for CHW programs across Texas. The certification would require 160 hours of classroom training and 1,000 hours of supervised field experience, with continuing education requirements.

How This Affects Your Life

If enacted, this bill would make it easier for community health workers in your neighborhood to receive standardized training and credentials, improving the quality of local health navigation services. It means more trusted, well-trained people helping you access healthcare, preventive screenings, and social services.

Guide

How to Become a Community Health Worker in Houston

Our Health Beginner Career

A step-by-step guide to certification, training programs, and career opportunities for aspiring community health workers in the Houston area.

1. Understanding the CHW Role

Community health workers (CHWs) are trusted members of the communities they serve. They help navigate healthcare systems, connect residents with preventive services, and provide culturally sensitive health education. In Texas, the CHW workforce has grown 40% since 2020.

2. Training and Certification

Texas requires 160 hours of classroom instruction plus 1,000 hours of supervised field experience. Programs are offered by several Houston-area organizations, with costs ranging from free (grant-funded) to $2,500.

3. Finding Employment

Houston-area hospitals, clinics, and nonprofits are actively hiring certified CHWs. Average starting salary ranges from $35,000 to $45,000, with experienced CHWs earning up to $55,000.

Houston Community Health Assessment 2025

156 pages 12.4 MB Published Dec 2025 Report

Summary

A comprehensive assessment of health conditions, access to care, and social determinants of health across Houston's diverse neighborhoods, with recommendations for community-driven health initiatives. The report draws on surveys from 12,000+ residents, hospital records, and census data to paint a detailed picture of Houston's health landscape.

Key Takeaways

  • Preventive care access varies dramatically by ZIP code
  • Community health worker programs show strongest ROI in underserved areas
  • Mental health screening rates increased 40% since 2023
  • Third Ward, Sunnyside, and Gulfton show greatest unmet need for health navigation
