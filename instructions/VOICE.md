## WHO THIS VOICE IS

David's voice is the opposite of nonprofit writing. Personal. Direct. Earned.
It moves fast. It trusts the reader. It never explains what it just said.

The closest analogy isn't a grant writer or a communications director.
It's a good memoirist who also happens to know how systems work.

---

## THE FIVE CORE TRAITS

### 1. Earned authority
Don't claim credibility — demonstrate it through specifics.
The plastic hangers. The $3. The 26 hours straight. The fifty kids in Nicaragua.
Every abstract claim has a concrete moment behind it.
If a sentence sounds like it's asserting something, find the detail that proves it instead.

### 2. Movement without transitions
No "Furthermore." No "In addition." No "As a result."
Just move. White space does the work of a transition.
Line breaks are punctuation. Section breaks are full stops.

### 3. Fragments as emphasis
Full sentences carry information. Fragments carry weight.
Deploy them after context has built, to land the point.

  ✓ "I stopped. Just like that."
  ✓ "No founder syndrome. Just love."
  ✓ "Opportunity." (single word, after a full paragraph of build-up)

### 4. Asset-based framing
Never write about what's broken.
Write about what exists, what's available, what's possible.
Even hard circumstances are written as doors that opened — not wounds that need healing.

  ✓ "We didn't build anything new. We just made what already exists findable."
  ✓ "Houston has everything. The organizations. The officials. The resources."

### 5. Inclusive "we"
"We" = the org alongside the community. Not the org serving the community from above.
The platform is shared infrastructure. The pronouns must reflect that.

  ✓ "We connect residents to what already exists."
  ✗ "We provide underserved communities with access to resources."

## PARAGRAPH LENGTH

- Most paragraphs: 1–3 sentences. Often 1.
- Long paragraphs build context. Short lines land the point.
- Short lines never come first. They follow the build.

Pattern:
  LONG → context, movement, setup (2–4 sentences)
  SHORT → the point
  FRAGMENT → the blow

Example:
  "I joined the Houston Food Bank to work on something bigger than food
  distribution — ending the conditions that make food banks necessary in
  the first place. I designed a program called Dining with Purpose. We
  brought people from all walks of life to the same table. Half the
  participants were food insecure. They shared meals and stories. They
  became part of something bigger."

  "They didn't just feel useful."

  "They felt seen."

## STRUCTURAL PATTERNS

### The three-line drop
A medium line. A shorter line. A fragment or single-word closer.
Often with line breaks between them.

  "The answer came fast."
  "Serve community."
  "From a place of love."

  "The problem was never motivation."
  (blank line)
  "It was always the on-ramp."

### Lists that aren't lists
When listing things, write them as prose fragments separated by periods — not bullets.
Bullets signal "comprehensive." This style signals "look at all this."

  ✓ "Photographer. Community organizer. Former co-founder of Spacetaker
     (now Fresh Arts) and the Houston Arts District. Builder of things
     that don't exist yet."

  ✓ "The organizations. The officials. The resources. The people doing the work."

Use bullet points only when items need to stand alone for scanability.
When you do, keep each bullet to subject + short clause. Not full descriptive sentences.

### Section architecture
1. Open with a grounding statement (place, person, moment, fact)
2. Build with 2–4 sentences of context or movement
3. Land with a short paragraph, fragment, or pull quote
4. Hard break. New section opens fresh — never explained, never announced.

### The critical narrative sequence (funder/story copy)
ALWAYS in this order:
1. Personal moment or observation (shows, doesn't tell)
2. Movement through experience (quick, no lingering)
3. Research/proof that validates what was already built
4. Platform as the infrastructure that scales the lesson
5. Invitation (never a hard sell)

CRITICAL: David built the prototype first. The research validated it after.
DO NOT write it as: "Research showed a need, so David built X."
WRITE IT AS: "David built X. The research confirmed it worked."

## FIVE SENTENCE-LEVEL MOVES

### Move 1: The abrupt pivot
Set up an expectation. Cut it short or subvert it. No transition word.

  ✓ "Demolition didn't last. I wasn't construction material."
  ✓ "I applied for a job at United Way. Didn't hear back."

### Move 2: The delayed payoff
End without the expected resolution. Land it after a beat.

  ✓ "I remember having $3 left after bills. I bought thirty plastic hangers."
     (pause / line break)
     "Hanging up my T-shirts felt like the height of luxury."

### Move 3: Repeated structure for accumulation
Parallel construction used for rhythm and weight, not rhetoric.

  ✓ "Not motivation. Not interest. Not willingness."
     "Opportunity."

  ✓ "The organizations. The officials. The resources. The people doing the work."

### Move 4: The plain declaration
State things plainly that others would hedge or qualify.
Trust is built by not adding disclaimers.

  ✓ "I stopped. Just like that."        ✗ "I made the difficult decision to step back."
  ✓ "No founder syndrome. Just love."   ✗ "I was committed to a smooth transition."
  ✓ "We didn't build anything new."     ✗ "Our innovative approach reimagines..."

### Move 5: The em dash as breath
Use em dashes for a pause, a qualification, or a mid-sentence turn.

  ✓ "A door that opened — and stayed open."
  ✓ "For the first time since I was 14, I paused. Really paused."

## VOCABULARY

### BANNED — NEVER USE THESE

| Never | Instead |
|-------|---------|
| underserved communities | neighbors / people / residents / Houstonians |
| stakeholders | people / partners / residents / community |
| leverage | use / build on / connect |
| impactful | concrete word for the actual impact |
| innovative / innovation | show what's different, don't name it |
| empower / empowerment | show the action |
| at-risk | describe the actual situation |
| wraparound services | the specific services |
| scalable / scale (as buzzword) | grow / replicate / run this in more places |
| ecosystem | community / network / web of orgs |
| capacity building | training / support / resources to grow |
| robust | delete it |
| seamless | delete it |
| comprehensive | show what's included instead |
| transformative | show the transformation |
| holistic | delete it — be specific |
| diverse / diversity | specific if at all: "Houstonians from all walks of life" |
| synergy | never |
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help / run / lead / create |
| I am passionate about | just say what you do |
| systems change | only if specific about what system and what change |
| paradigm | never |

### DAVID'S ACTUAL VOCABULARY — USE THESE

- on-ramp / the door / the way in — metaphors for access
- already exists — asset framing
- findable — specific, humble, true
- show up — action, not aspiration
- neighbors — preferred over "community members" or "residents"
- from a place of love — used deliberately, never casually
- quiet — for acts of kindness that don't ask for attention
- just — plain declaration ("I stopped. Just like that.")
- really — emphasis without exclamation ("I paused. Really paused.")
- the next right thing — action over planning
- building things that don't exist yet — David's self-description

### NUMBERS — ALWAYS EXACT

Specific numbers read as true. Round numbers read as estimates.

  ✓ "$3" not "less than five dollars"
  ✓ "thirty plastic hangers" not "a few hangers"
  ✓ "517 organizations" not "over 500"
  ✓ "164 focus areas" — exact, always
  ✓ "26 hours straight" not "over a day"
  ✓ "50 neighborhoods" — exact

## CONTENT MODES

### Mode 1: Personal Narrative
(About page, founder statements, Substack, keynote openings)

- Open in scene — person, place, or moment. No thesis statement upfront.
- Move through time quickly. No lingering in difficulty.
- Named people, specific jobs, real amounts of money.
- Transformation shown through a hinge moment, not explained.
- End with an opening — invitation, door, beginning — not a conclusion.

### Mode 2: Funder / Platform Copy
(Partner page, grant narratives, one-pagers, funder emails)

- Open with who this is for — the person who needs it, not the problem.
- Proof before vision. What's built, then where it's going.
- Research cited as validation, not as the reason the work started.
- Investment levels framed as what they fund, not what they "unlock."
- Close with an invitation. Never a hard CTA.
  ✓ "Let's talk." ✗ "Invest today to transform communities."

### Mode 3: Civic / Platform Voice
(Site pages, pathway descriptions, resource categories, UI copy)

- Second person ("you") when addressing the user directly.
- Plain language — written for a 6th-grade reading level, not dumbed down.
- Zero jargon. If you wouldn't say it in conversation, don't write it.
- Action-oriented. What can the reader do from here?
- Acknowledge that finding things is hard — without blaming the person.

  ✓ "Find the people and organizations already working on this."
  ✗ "Explore our comprehensive database of community resources."

  ✓ "Your neighbors are already doing this work."
  ✗ "Community members are actively engaged in addressing these systemic issues."

### Mode 4: Social / Short-Form
(LinkedIn, Instagram, Substack notes, email subjects)

- One idea. One paragraph. Maybe two.
- Open with the sharpest version of the point. No preamble.
- Delete "I've been thinking about..." and start with what you were thinking about.
- End on a question or an open line. Invites response.
- No hashtags unless platform requires. No emojis.

  ✓ "Most people want to connect. The #1 barrier is a lack of opportunity."
  ✓ "Division is not inevitable. It's a design problem."

## AI WRITING TELLS — FLAG AND FIX THESE

When AI drafts in this voice, these patterns appear. Catch them every time.

### Tell 1: Transition word openers
DELETE on sight: Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover, However (sentence start),
In addition, As a result, Therefore, Thus, Notably, Importantly.

  ✗ "Furthermore, the scope of the work expanded..."
  ✓ (just start the next sentence)

### Tell 2: The explanation after the moment
AI adds a sentence after a good specific moment explaining what it meant.
DELETE IT. The moment speaks for itself.

  ✗ "Hanging up my T-shirts felt like the height of luxury. This experience
     taught me the profound value of small comforts in difficult times."
  ✓ "Hanging up my T-shirts felt like the height of luxury." (stop there)

### Tell 3: "I am committed to" / "I remain dedicated to"
  ✗ "I remain deeply committed to supporting the organization's success."
  ✓ "No founder syndrome. Just love."

### Tell 4: Passive voice
  ✗ "The program was studied by More in Common as part of their research."
  ✓ "More in Common studied what we built."

### Tell 5: Adjective stacks
Maximum one adjective per noun. Usually zero. Let the noun do the work.
  ✗ "An innovative, evidence-based, community-centered program that
     meaningfully engaged diverse stakeholders."
  ✓ "A program that brought people together over food."

### Tell 6: The banner closer
Any closing that sounds like it belongs on a conference banner is wrong.
  ✗ "Together, we can build a more connected, equitable, and vibrant Houston for all."
  ✓ "Let's get to work."

## PRE-PUBLISH CHECKLIST

Run every piece of copy through this before it ships.

STRUCTURE
☐ Does every section open in scene, moment, or specific fact?
☐ Are paragraphs 1–3 sentences (with rare exceptions)?
☐ Is there at least one fragment or short punchy line per section?
☐ Do transitions happen through white space, not transition words?

VOICE
☐ Any jargon? (See banned word list above)
☐ Any sentence explaining what the previous sentence already showed?
☐ Does "we" include the community or talk about them from outside?
☐ Any adjective stacks (more than one adjective on the same noun)?
☐ Any sentence starting with Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover, However?

SPECIFICITY
☐ Are numbers exact where they should be?
☐ At least one concrete detail (person, amount, place, action) per section?
☐ Can any abstract claim be replaced with a specific example?

ASSET FRAMING
☐ Is the copy describing what's broken, or what's possible?
☐ Are the people in the copy agents, or recipients?
☐ Does the platform come across as infrastructure, not charity?

CLOSING
☐ Does the piece end open (invitation, beginning) not closed (conclusion)?
☐ Is the closing line specific to this piece, not generic to any org?
☐ If there's a CTA: invitation ("Let's talk") not command ("Donate now")?

## QUICK REFERENCE EXAMPLES

These are the gold standard. When in doubt, read these and match the register.

ABOUT PAGE OPENER:
  "We could start this story anywhere. I'll start with me at 15."

FUNDER PROOF:
  "I didn't read the research and decide to build something. I built the
  prototype. The research validated it. Now I'm building the infrastructure
  to scale it."

PLATFORM VALUE PROP:
  "We didn't build anything new. We just made what already exists findable."

HERO HEADLINE:
  "THIS IS THE WAY IN."

STAT FRAMING:
  "517 organizations. 44 elected officials. 25 services. 269 resources.
  Everything here already existed. We just made it findable."

FUNDER CLOSE:
  "Houston has everything. The organizations. The officials. The resources.
  The people doing the work. We just made it findable. Let's talk."

PULL QUOTE (universal):
  "They didn't just feel useful. They felt seen."
