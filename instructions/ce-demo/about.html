Civic intelligence for the 18-county Houston region — and a model for cities across the country.

What this is

Houston is one of the most complex cities in America. Diverse, decentralized, fast-moving. The organizations doing the most important work here — in food access, housing, health, education, environmental justice — often can't find each other. The people who need them most often can't find them at all.

Community Exchange is the connective tissue. For any issue you care about, it shows you the organizations doing the work, the officials who hold power over it, the services available right now, and the policies in play. It doesn't create new resources. It makes the ones that already exist findable.

Everything here already existed. The way in is new.

The platform covers 517 organizations, 44 elected officials, 228 policies, 164 focus areas, and 25 services across 18 counties — with new data added daily through automated pipelines and community submissions.

How it works

The Change Engine

Behind Community Exchange is the Change Engine — a classification system that maps every organization, official, policy, and service to a shared set of 164 focus areas across 7 pathways, crosswalked to federal taxonomy systems including AIRS/211, NTEE, UN SDGs, and social determinants of health.

That shared taxonomy is what makes the connections visible. When you look at an organization, you can immediately see which officials oversee the issues it works on, which policies are in play, and which other organizations are doing related work. The data was always there. The structure is new.

The pathways

Seven pathways organize everything on the platform: Our Health, Our Homes, Our Schools, Our Work, Our Environment, Our Voice, and Our Safety. Each pathway is a door into the full network. Each one maps to the issues real people face — not the administrative categories government uses to organize services.

Ask Chance

Chance is the platform's AI assistant — built on the Change Engine's data, trained to help people navigate the system in plain language. If you're not sure where to start, Chance is a good first stop.

Who built this

Community Exchange is built by The Change Lab, a Houston-based systems-change practice founded by David A. Brown. The Change Lab is fiscally sponsored by Impact Hub Houston, a 501(c)(3) organization.

The Change Lab's tagline is "Community life, organized." That's what this is — an attempt to organize the existing assets of civic life in one of America's most complex cities, so that more people can actually use them.

Partner with us

Community Exchange is infrastructure. It's built to be sustainable, open, and expandable — first in Houston, then elsewhere. We're actively seeking foundation partners, civic technology collaborators, and government partners who want to connect their data to the network.
