## ABOUT PAGE — /about

### Full Page Copy [APPROVED]

  Page headline: About the Community Exchange
  Subhead: Community life, organized.

  —

  We could start this story anywhere.

  I'll start with me at 15.

  Super depressed. Newly homeless. Crashing wherever I could — chaise
  lounges at apartment complexes, couches if I was lucky, the occasional
  playground bench when I wasn't.

  By day, I did demolition work. Gutting mid-rise office buildings. Taking
  turns jumping through studs like low-budget stunt doubles.

  Demolition didn't last. I wasn't construction material.

  —

  What came next: cleaning concert halls, a plastics factory from 6 AM to
  6 PM, six days a week. Foggy air — literally and figuratively.

  That's when the McCullys stepped in. Their son and I had reconnected.
  They quietly offered me a place to land.

  For the first time in a long time, a door had opened — and stayed open.

  I remember having $3 left after bills. I bought thirty plastic hangers.

  [PULL QUOTE] Hanging up my T-shirts felt like the height of luxury.

  —

  Restaurants became my world. 15 years in the back of house. At 17, I had
  health insurance.

  Around then, I was still making questionable decisions. But one day it
  hit me: this isn't who I want to be.

  [PULL QUOTE] I stopped. Just like that.

  I moved to Texas. Enrolled at Houston Community College even though I
  didn't feel like I belonged. Two professors took me under their wing.
  One invited me to help rebuild an orphanage in Nicaragua.

  We launched ESL programs, repaired buildings, and met fifty kids who
  lived in deep poverty but carried joy like it was their birthright.

  [PULL QUOTE] That trip cracked my world open.

  —

  Back in Houston: University of Houston, studio art, the Sculpture Society,
  a gala, a board president who became my wife.

  That work grew into Spacetaker — a 3,000 sq. ft. artist resource center.
  I sold my truck to file the nonprofit paperwork. Over time, we helped
  artists generate over a million dollars in commissions and sales.
  Spacetaker is still going today as Fresh Arts.

  Then the Houston Arts District — connecting the historically divided First
  and Sixth Wards. Gained state recognition. Then handed it off.

  No founder syndrome. Just love.

  —

  Photography became full-time work. Clients: Microsoft, LinkedIn, the
  Greater Houston Partnership, The Menil.

  Then the pandemic hit. My business collapsed. For the first time since I
  was 14, I paused. Really paused.

  And I asked myself: What do I want to do with the rest of my life?

  The answer came fast: Serve community. From a place of love.

  —

  I joined the Houston Food Bank to work on something bigger than food
  distribution — ending the conditions that make food banks necessary in
  the first place.

  I designed a program called Dining with Purpose. We brought people from
  all walks of life to the same table. Half the participants were food
  insecure. They shared meals and stories. They became part of something
  bigger.

  They didn't just feel useful.

  [PULL QUOTE] They felt seen.

  More in Common — one of the country's leading research organizations on
  social connection — studied what we built. Their two-year study surveyed
  6,000 Americans.

  Their findings:
  — 90% of participants felt a stronger sense of connection to their community
  — The program was named a national model for cross-difference connection
  — Houston outperformed national averages on every measure of willingness to connect

  I didn't read the research and decide to build something.

  [PULL QUOTE] I built the prototype. The research validated it. Now I'm
  building the infrastructure to scale it.
  — David A. Brown, Founder

  —

  Here's what More in Common found after surveying 6,000 Americans:

  70% feel a responsibility to connect across difference.
  The #1 barrier: a lack of opportunity.

  Not motivation. Not interest. Not willingness.

  Opportunity.

  That's what the Community Exchange is. Opportunity — organized. For
  anyone who wants to help but doesn't know where to start. For anyone
  who needs something but doesn't know what exists. For anyone who cares
  about Houston and wants to show up.

  We connect residents to the officials, services, organizations, and
  opportunities that already exist. In their language. At their level.
  In their neighborhood.

  [PULL QUOTE] We didn't build anything new. We just made what already
  exists findable.

  —

  WHAT WE'VE BUILT

  517 Organizations
  44 Elected Officials
  25 Social Services
  269 Resources
  7 Community Pathways
  18 Counties
  3 Languages (English · Spanish · Vietnamese)

  —

  WHO WE ARE

  The Community Exchange is a project of The Change Lab, a Houston-based
  civic infrastructure practice founded in 2025. We are fiscally sponsored
  by Impact Hub Houston, a 501(c)(3).

  David A. Brown is the founder and Director of Possibility of The Change
  Lab. Photographer. Community organizer. Former co-founder of Spacetaker
  (now Fresh Arts) and the Houston Arts District. Designer of the Dining
  with Purpose prototype. Builder of things that don't exist yet.

## PARTNER PAGE — /partner

### Full Page Copy [APPROVED]

  Page headline: This is infrastructure. Want to help build it?

  —

  Most civic platforms are built for activists. People who already know
  the vocabulary, already know the organizations, already know how to
  find their way in.

  We built this for everyone else.

  The neighbors who care but feel like there's no on-ramp. The residents
  who need something — food, housing, legal help, a doctor — and don't
  know what exists two miles away.

  That's who this is for.

  —

  WHAT WE'VE PROVEN

  This isn't a hypothesis.

  In 2023, I designed Dining with Purpose at the Houston Food Bank —
  shared meals, diverse stakeholders, one common challenge: food insecurity.
  Half the people at the table were food insecure themselves.

  More in Common studied it as part of a two-year national research project.
  Their findings:

  — 90% of participants felt a stronger sense of connection to their community
  — The program was cited as a national model for cross-difference connection
  — Houston outperformed national averages on every measure of willingness to connect

  The research confirmed what we already knew from doing the work:
  people want to show up.

  [PULL QUOTE] They just need the door to be open.

  The Community Exchange is that door. At scale.

  —

  WHAT WE'RE BUILDING

  Right now — live at changeengine.us:
  517 organizations. 44 elected officials. 25 social services. 269 resources.
  7 community pathways. 18 counties. English, Spanish, and Vietnamese.
  A civic AI guide. Election information. A polling place finder. A research
  library. Volunteer opportunities. Civic action tools.

  It's already real. It's already being used.

  By end of 2026:
  — Full Change Engine launch — 164 focus areas mapped to 7 themes and 17 UN SDGs
  — Mobile-optimized experience
  — Neighborhood-level data for all 50 Houston neighborhoods
  — 211 API integration for real-time service availability
  — Partnerships with anchor institutions across the 18-county region
  — National replication framework — any city can run this model

  2027:
  The first city outside Houston runs this model.

  —

  —

  WHY NOW

  Division is not inevitable. It's a design problem.

  The research says 70% of Americans want to connect across difference.
  The #1 barrier is a lack of opportunity. We're removing that barrier —
  in the most diverse city in the country, with a platform built to be
  replicated everywhere else.

  Houston has everything. The organizations. The officials. The resources.
  The people doing the work.

  [PULL QUOTE] We just made it findable.
